What to Charge for Custom Leather Work

I let customers change anything they wanted. Here is what that cost — and the business I should have built instead.

By Chad Gill — Gill’s Custom Leather. I built and sold custom leather sheaths, detecting rigs, and pouches while working full time as a firefighter. Everything below comes from running that shop.

If you are about to take your first commission, the question you are searching for is what to charge. I will answer it. But the more useful answer is the one nobody gave me: the scope you offer decides how much work you do, and how much of it you never get paid for.

How much should I charge for a custom leather sheath?

Materials plus your hourly rate times the hours — and then a separate line for every customization, at no less than $40–50 each.

Materials on a knife sheath are cheap and remarkably stable. A side of 6–7 oz veg-tan runs $60–80 and yields roughly ten sheaths. Thread, glue, and dye add a few dollars. Call it twelve dollars a sheath, whatever the blade.

Time is where the money is, and time is what beginners give away. Six or seven hours at the bench is normal for a well-made sheath with a molded fit, burnished edges, and hand-stitching. Pick an hourly rate you would accept from anyone else and multiply.

Then charge for customization separately, every single time.

Why charge extra for initials, logos, and design changes?

Because a traced logo costs the same hours whether the sheath is small or large — it has nothing to do with the size of the job, and everything to do with the size of the bill.

I offered customization on everything. Tooling patterns. Initials. A customer’s own logo, which I would trace out by hand. A smaller pouch. A snap instead of a magnetic clasp. Each request was reasonable on its own. Each one was also a fresh design problem, solved once, for one person, and never reused.

None of it was priced. I folded hours of one-off design work into a $95 sheath because the customer asked nicely and I wanted them to be happy.

If you take that work on, price it as its own line item. Forty to fifty dollars is a floor, not a ceiling, and a logo you have never seen before should cost more than that. The number is not about greed. It is about making sure the request is worth making.

Should I take custom orders or build in batches?

Batches — five to ten near-identical pieces, cut and built together, sold from stock.

Experienced makers already work this way, and it is not a secret. What is rarely written down is what the alternative costs. Every custom order is its own setup: a new pattern, a new layout, a new decision about hardware, an email thread to settle the details, and a promise about a date. None of that work exists in a batch.

Cut ten backs and ten fronts at once. Wet-mold them in sequence. Dye them in one session. You amortize every setup across ten pieces, and the tenth one is faster and better than the first because your hands have just done it nine times.

Then sell what exists. A finished sheath ships tomorrow. A promised sheath has to be built, and until it is, you owe someone something.

What happens if you say yes to everything?

You end up with a waiting list you cannot clear.

Mine reached six to nine months, and at times I could not deliver even inside that. I was working full time as a firefighter and raising a family, and every accepted customization added hours to a queue that was already too long. The craft was never the problem. I loved the bench. The problem was that I had defined a product with no edges, and a product with no edges cannot be scheduled.

A fixed catalogue solves this before it starts. When the thing you sell is a specific sheath in a specific configuration, there is nothing to negotiate and nothing to wait for. You decide what you make. You make ten of them. You sell them.

Is it worth doing at all?

Yes — if you scope it so the work stays the part you love.

I have built in leather, in wood, and in metal, and the satisfaction is the same in all three: you make a thing, it is well made, and someone is glad they own it. A sheath I built years ago is on somebody’s belt right now, and it has not failed him.

What burns people out is not the bench. It is everything bolted onto the bench — the design requests, the status emails, the queue, the promises. Define a product with edges. Build ten of them. Sell what exists.
